Fmax Normalization

Fmax normalization is applied by selecting the checkbox ("Fmax Normalize") located just above the explorer panel tree, while deselecting the checkbox removes Fmax normalization. Note also that Fmax normalization is applied to all runs contained within the database.

Fmax normalization corrects for well-to-well differences in fluorescence calibration, that is, differences in the fluorescent scale assigned by the instrument to each individual well. Although the impact of such variance generally small , the simplistic natural of Fmax normalization makes it an attractive option for potentially increasing the accuracy of LRE quantification, which can be significant in some cases. As discussed below, analysis of a run's average Fmax also introduces an additional quality control component that is available whether Fmax normalization is implemented or not.

So, how can of fluorescence calibration be analyzed, and how does it impact quantitative accuracy? Due to the fact that LRE determines target quantity expressed fluorescence units, quantitative accuracy is directly dependent on the optical precision of the qPCR instrument. Extensive application of optical calibration using lambda gDNA has historically indicated that for most modern qPCR instruments, this is not a major concern. However, closer examination has revealed that significant well-to-well differences in fluorescent scale can occur, despite the high levels of optical precision that these instruments can generate.

Although still under investigation, such variances have been found to generate quantitative errors in the range of 0.5- to 2.0-fold, based on the quantitative differences observed between identical samples amplified within different wells. Further examination revealed that these quantitative differences correlate directly to the height of each respective profile. In the absence of any other factor (see below), differences in profile height directly reflects differences in the fluorescence units assigned by the instrument to each respective well (referred to as fluorescence calibration).

Under the LRE model, quantifying profile height is an integral part of target quantification. Defined as the maximal fluorescence, Fmax, this provides a quantitative approach to addressing variances in fluorescence calibration, in addition to providing a run-to-run quality control component. This is accomplished by first determining the average Fmax across all wells within each run. The key aspect here, is that this provides a reference to which the Fmax generated by individual wells can be compared.

For example, if a profile generates a Fmax that is 50% higher than the average Fmax , it will generate ~50% overestimation of target quantity (i.e. the fluorescence units are 50% greater than the average). When Fmax normalization is applied, this bias is corrected by simply lowering the predicted target quantity by 50%. If the profile height is smaller than the average, the target quantity is similarly increased in relation to the average Fmax .

 The average Fmax also provides a simple method for assessing the level of fluorescence unit variation within a run, as reflected by the coefficient of  variation (CV = standard deviation / average X 100%). Typically, a run generates a Fmax CV in the range of 10-15%, which reflects the level of quantitative variance that would be generated (fluorescence units have a linear scale), assuming the absence of other factors. It is here, however, that another aspect must be considered, which is that differences in amplification efficiency (Emax) also impacts profile height.

A necessary assumption of Fmax normalization, as it is applied in this version of the LRE Analyzer, is that amplification efficiency is equal, or at least similar, for all profiles within a run. Maybe it would be best to conduct analysis of the lambda runs in relation to variance in Emax, and of a typical run(s) to better determine that actual variance in Emax across different amplicons, before completing this synopsis.

The average Fmax also allows run-to-run differences in fluorescence scale to be assessed, by comparing their respective average Fmax values. The average Fmax across all runs contained within a database is presented in the top node within the profile explorer panel, along with the corresponding CV. Although these differences are generally small (<10%), this does allow any aberrations in the optical characteristic of a run to be easily identified. This could be produced, for example, by technical errors in reaction setup or aberrations in an instrument's optical system. Although such situations are expected to be rare, it does provide another quality control component that describes the optical characteristics of individual runs.
