Fmax Normalization

Fmax normalization is applied by selecting the "Fmax Normalize" checkbox located just above the explorer panel tree, while deselecting the checkbox removes Fmax normalization. In addition, an asterisk (*) is added to the profile label to indicate that Fmax formalization has been applied.

Fmax normalization was introduced primarily as an attempt to correct for well-to-well differences in fluorescence scale generated by the instrument's optical system. Although such variances are generally small , the simplistic natural of Fmax normalization provides an attractive option for potentially increasing the accuracy of LRE quantification. As discussed below, analysis of a run's average Fmax also introduces an additional quality control component.

Because target quantity is derived from the fluorescence readings, the quantitative accuracy of LRE is directly dependent on the optical precision of the qPCR instrument. In particular, LRE quantification assumes that all wells have identical optical characteristics. However, as illustrated below, large variations in profile height brings this assumption into question.

Identical quantities of lambda gDNA were amplified using the CAL1 amplicon, which generated an average Fmax of 4,290,000. Importantly, these differences in profile height are linearly related to differences in predicted target quantity, and is a result of variances in the optics of each well. In this case, the two most extreme profiles generate close to a two fold difference in predicted target quantity (high vs. low = 2,866 vs. 1,537; actual quantity = 1,876 molecules).

Although still under investigation, in the absence of other factors (see below), differences in profile height directly reflect differences in the fluorescence units assigned by the instrument's optical system to each respective well, referred to here as fluorescence calibration.

Under the LRE model, quantifying profile height is an integral part of target quantification. Defined as the maximal fluorescence, Fmax, this provides a quantitative approach to addressing variances in fluorescence calibration, in addition to providing a run-to-run quality control component. This is accomplished by first determining the average Fmax across all wells within each run. The key aspect here, is that this provides a reference to which the Fmax generated by individual wells can be compared.

For example, if a profile generates a Fmax that is 50% higher than the average Fmax , it will generate ~50% overestimation of target quantity (i.e. the fluorescence units are 50% greater than the average). When Fmax normalization is applied, this bias is corrected by simply lowering the predicted target quantity by 50%. If the profile height is smaller than the average, the target quantity is similarly increased in relation to the average Fmax .

The average Fmax also provides a simple method for assessing the level of fluorescence unit variation within a run, as reflected by the coefficient of  variation (CV = standard deviation / average X100%). Typically, a run generates a Fmax CV in the range of ±15%, which reflects the general level of quantitative variance that would be generated (fluorescence units have a linear scale and impact target quantity in a linear fashion). Importantly, this assumes the absence of other factors. It is here, however, that another aspect must be considered, which is that differences in amplification efficiency (Emax) also impacts Fmax (Rutledge and Stewart 2010).

A necessary assumption of Fmax normalization, as it is applied in this version of the LRE Analyzer, is that amplification efficiency is equal, or at least similar, for all profiles within a run. A second assumption is that all amplicons generate similar fluorescence intensities, which based on previous work (Rutledge and Stewart 2010), appears to be generally correct. However, more work is required to better understand this phenomenon. For example, the SAND F1R1 amplicon that is included in the demonstration experiment database, consistently generates Fmax values that are about 25% lower that the average Fmax, the reason for which is currently undetermined.

Finally, the average Fmax also allows run-to-run differences in fluorescence scale to be assessed, by comparing their respective average Fmax values. The average Fmax across all runs contained within a database, along with the corresponding CV, is presented as a hint within the top node within the profile explorer panel (made visible by hovering the mouse over the node), . Although these differences are generally small (<5%), this does allow any aberrations in the optical characteristic of an individual run to be easily identified. This could be produced, for example, by technical errors in reaction setup or aberrations in an instrument's optical system. Although such situations are expected to be rare, it does provide quality control component that describes the optical characteristics of individual runs, along the that of individual wells as discussed above.
