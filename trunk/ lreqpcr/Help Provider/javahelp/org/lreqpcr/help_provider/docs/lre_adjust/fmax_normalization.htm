Fmax Normalization

Fmax normalization is applied by selecting the "Fmax Normalize" checkbox located just above the explorer panel tree, while deselecting the checkbox removes Fmax normalization. In addition, an asterisk (*) is added to the profile label to indicate that Fmax formalization has been applied.

Fmax normalization was introduced primarily to correct for well-to-well differences in fluorescence scale generated by the instrument's optical system. Although such variances are generally small , the simple natural of Fmax normalization provides an attractive option for potentially increasing the accuracy of LRE quantification. As discussed below, analysis of a run's average Fmax also introduces an additional quality control component.

Because target quantity is derived from the fluorescence readings within the central region of a profile, the quantitative accuracy of LRE is directly dependent on the optical precision of the qPCR instrument. In particular, LRE quantification assumes that all wells have identical optical characteristics. However, as illustrated below, large differences in profile height brings this assumption into question.

Identical quantities of lambda gDNA were amplified using the CAL1 amplicon, which generated an average Fmax of 4,290,000. Importantly, differences in profile height are linearly related to differences in predicted target quantity, and is a result of variances in the optics of each respective well. In this case, the two most extreme profiles generate close to a two fold difference in predicted target quantity (high vs. low = 2,866 vs. 1,537; actual quantity = 1,876 molecules).

Although still under investigation, in the absence of other factors (see below), differences in profile height directly reflect differences in the fluorescence scale assigned to each respective well by the instrument's optical system, referred to here as fluorescence calibration.

Under the LRE model, quantifying profile height is an integral part of target quantification. Defined as the maximal fluorescence, Fmax provides a quantitative approach to addressing differences in fluorescence calibration, in addition to providing a run-to-run quality control component. This is accomplished by first determining the average Fmax across all wells within each run. The key aspect here, is that this provides a reference to which the Fmax generated by an individual well can be compared.

For example, if a profile generates a Fmax that is 50% higher than the average Fmax , it will generate a 50% overestimation of target quantity (i.e. the fluorescence units are 50% greater than the average). When Fmax normalization is applied, this bias is corrected by simply lowering the predicted target quantity by 50%. If the profile height is smaller than the average, the target quantity is similarly increased in relation to the average Fmax .

The average Fmax also provides a simple method for assessing the variance of fluorescence calibration across all wells within a run, as expressed by the coefficient of  variation (CV = standard deviation / average X100%). Importantly, this assumes the absence of other factors, which is not necessarily the case. For example, differences in amplification efficiency (Emax) also impacts Fmax in a linear fashion (Rutledge and Stewart 2010).

Thus, a necessary assumption of Fmax normalization, as it is applied in this version of the LRE Analyzer, is that amplification efficiency is equal, or at least similar, for all profiles within a run. A second assumption is that all amplicons generate similar fluorescence intensities, which based on previous work (Rutledge and Stewart 2010), appears to be generally correct. However, more work is required to better understand this phenomenon. For example, the SAND F1R1 amplicon that is included in the demonstration experiment database, for unknown reasons consistently generates Fmax values that are about 25% lower than the average Fmax. Although technically difficult to implement, future versions of the program could encompass the average Fmax generated by an individual amplicon. However, note that in addition to being small, such systemic biases are repeatable, such that relative differences, e.g., between samples, are not impacted.

Finally, the average Fmax also allows run-to-run differences in fluorescence calibration to be assessed, by comparing their respective average Fmax values. The average Fmax across all runs contained within a database, along with the corresponding CV, is presented as in the top node label. Although these run-to-run variances are generally small (<5%), this does allow any aberrations in the optical characteristic of an individual run to be easily identified. This could be produced, for example, by technical errors in reaction setup or aberrations in an instrument's optical system during a run. Although such situations are expected to be rare, it does provide quality control component based on the optical characteristics of individual runs, along with that of individual wells as discussed above.
